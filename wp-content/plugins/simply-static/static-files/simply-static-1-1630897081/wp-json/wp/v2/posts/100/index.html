There’s a new bug<\/a> invading the eastern U.S.<\/a>\u00a0<\/p>\n

The spotted lanternfly has been seen in New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania and Indiana.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Rhode Island discovered the state’s first specimen at the beginning of the month, state environmental<\/a> officials said.<\/p>\n

INVISIBLE FLESH-EATING MITES TERRORIZE DC RESIDENTS<\/strong><\/a><\/p>\n

The winged pest, also known as Lycorma delicatula<\/i>, is native to China<\/a>.\u00a0<\/p>\n

It was first detected in Pennsylvania in 2014 but appeared to have already been present for two to three years prior, according to the USDA National Invasive Species Information Center<\/a>.<\/p>\n

It has also been found in Delaware, Maryland, Connecticut and Virginia, according to the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC)<\/a>.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The spotted lanternfly appears as big as a butterfly but with a moth-like appearance.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture states<\/a> that an adult is approximately 1 inch long and half an inch wide at rest, with tan, semi-transparent forewings, black spots, patches of red and black and a white band and yellow and black abdomen.<\/p>\n

The spotted lanternfly’s immature stages are black with white spots, and they develop the red patches as they mature.<\/p>\n

Adults appear starting in July and in the fall adults lay 30 to 50 1-inch-long egg masses on tree trunks, firewood, rocks and vehicles.\u00a0<\/p>\n

WEST NILE VIRUS REPORTED IN MULTIPLE STATES<\/strong><\/a><\/p>\n

The periodical cicadas in the eastern U.S. also laid their eggs in trees, so why is the spotted lanternfly “Public Enemy Number One?”<\/p>\n

The USDA National Invasive Species Information Center says it poses a “serious economic<\/a> threat to multiple U.S. industries.”<\/p>\n

The insect feeds on agricultural crops<\/a> like grapes, apples and hops as well as maple, walnut and willow trees.<\/p>\n

The Empire State says its presence could impact forests \u2013 making plants vulnerable to disease \u2013 \u00a0and the agricultural and tourism industries.<\/p>\n

New York’s annual yield of apples and grapes \u2013 a combined value of $358.4 million \u2013 could be “greatly impacted” by infestations, the DEC said.<\/p>\n

Signs of infestation include sap oozing from tree trunks; 1-inch-long, brownish-gray or brown and scaly egg masses; and honeydew build-up under plants. The lanternflies excrete honeydew, which encourages the growth of black sooty mold.<\/p>\n

Multiple counties in Pennsylvania are now quarantined<\/u><\/a>, the Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Reports show<\/a> New Jersey’s Somerset and Hunterdon counties are also under quarantine.<\/p>\n

The message to people who believe they’ve seen these insects is clear: If you see something, say something\u2026after you kill it.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“Kill it! Squash it, smash it…just get rid of it,” the Pennsylvania Department of Agriculture instructed.<\/p>\n

“If you see a Spotted Lanternfly, help us Stomp it Out,” New Jersey’s Department of Agriculture pleaded<\/a>.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Officials also encourage all “SLF” spotters and killers to submit public reports using state websites to be able to better understand the spread of this species.<\/p>\n
