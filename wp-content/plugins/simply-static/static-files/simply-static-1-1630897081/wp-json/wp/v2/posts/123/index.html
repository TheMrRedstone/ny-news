The U.S. war in Afghanistan<\/a> basically ended this past week.\u00a0After 20 years, America\u2019s longest war came\u00a0 to a close with the last U.S.<\/a> troops exiting Kabul.<\/p>\n

But U.S. service members<\/a> around the world were still very busy, evacuating Americans, Afghans and others out of the clutches of the new Taliban<\/a> rulers. Including those stationed at the massive American Ramstein Airbase in Germany,<\/a> a key transit hub for those leaving Afghanistan and going on to the U.S.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“It\u2019s been pretty much nonstop,” Captain James Dimmick, who\u2019s managing air operations at the base, told Fox News. “We\u2019ve been 24 hours every day of the week so far. \u2026 Just getting as many people in and out as we possibly can.”<\/p>\n

LAST US TROOPS HAVE DEPARTED AFGHANISTAN<\/strong><\/a><\/p>\n

Huge C-17 transport planes flew the Afghans directly in from Kabul or via other bases in places like Qatar.\u00a0 Then after the refugees are checked out and vetted they board specially-chartered commercial planes to fly on to the States.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The commander of Ramstein, Brig. General Joshua Olson, told us it\u2019s all about “\u2026 humanity and providing hope.”\u00a0<\/p>\n

Sometimes those flying in are not in great shape.\u00a0Like the 20 injured Marines and other service members medevaced there following the deadly ISIS terror attack at Kabul\u2019s airport. They were treated at the nearby American military hospital, Landstuhl Regional Medical Center. Some, too, were near death. All were treated until well enough to be flown back to Walter Reed Medical Center in the States.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“They can go out and do what they need to do \u2026 what their country is asking them to do” Col. Jodelle Schroeder, head of nursing at the hospital, told Fox News, “because we\u2019ve got their back.”<\/p>\n

As for the Afghans themselves, they had mixed feelings when we spoke with them but were grateful to be out of the chaos of the country.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0<\/p>\n

“I think the country is going the wrong way,” one told Fox News. “It will not be the same as it was.”\u00a0<\/p>\n

“I don\u2019t think the future will be very good,” another said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

They have no illusions about the new Taliban rulers after having seen what the extreme Islamists did 20 years ago when they last ran the country.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“We have history with the Taliban,” an Afghan asserted. “We saw them once before \u2026 enemies never change.”\u00a0<\/p>\n

And many were worried about the thousands left behind, including family members, all potentially in harm\u2019s way following the rapid U.S. withdrawal and evacuation.<\/p>\n

“My brother, my family, all the people who worked for the U.S. military, they are still there,” yet another Afghan explained. “They\u2019re stranded.”\u00a0<\/p>\n

We asked commanding General Olson if he had wanted more time. “We always want more time,” he told us, “more time \u2026 and more sleep!”<\/p>\n

As for this period representing the end of America\u2019s war in Afghanistan, most of the service members there were also too busy to spend much time reflecting on it. But several told us that the nearly 20 years of fighting there basically tracked their whole career in the military. There is a personal stake.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“Was it all worth it?” I asked Germany-posted Lt. Col Elizabeth Dwyer.\u00a0 “Absolutely,” she replied.\u00a0<\/p>\n

“Saving lives?” I suggested.\u00a0\u00a0<\/p>\n

“That\u2019s what we do,” she stated emphatically.\u00a0<\/p>\n

They do that \u2026 and a lot more. Political debate aside, on vivid and emotional display to us at the crucial U.S. military hub in Germany in the past week, among Americans and Afghans: dedication, relief, concern \u2026 and, yes, humanity.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0<\/p>\n
